Overview

As human lifespan increases, the potential for mistakes to occur during cell replication also increases. Due to the increase in quality of life, especially in America, cancer has become the leading cause of death. In 2012 alone, there were "14.1 million new cases and 8.2 million cancer-related deaths worldwide" (Cancer Statistics). As the cases of cancer rises worldwide, mortality rates rise with it. However, as cancer has become a more prevalent disease in our everyday lives, we have developed better testing methods to follow.

The CDC has collected data from each state from 1999 to 2016 on the incidence of cancer and moratlity from it. The data is available for download at this link; in addition, the CDC has created their own visualizations. These graphs are available here. However, the graphs availabe on thir website only consist of data from 2016. The graphs below encompass all incidence and mortality rates from 1999 to 2016 for all races.

General Data Analysis

Much of the analysis here is the same; California has the largest state population in the United States at 39 million people. Texas follows second at 29 million people , and Florida coming in third at 21 million people. As a result, higher numbers of people mean a higher incidence and mortality rate of cancer. This can be seen in every graph shown below, with California, Texas, and Florida outmatching nearly every other state. In some cases New York shows a presence.

The first map shows an incidence rate for both males and females, for all races. California has the highest incidence rate at approximately 826,000 cases since 1999; Florida follows with just under 610,000 incidence rates. While California has a much higher total incidence rate, a simple explanation for this is that their population is much higher than any other state.

The second map shows the mortality rate for males and females of all races. Again, California has the highest mortality rate at approximately 293,000 deaths. Much like incidence rate, this is most easily explained because the California population is much higher than that of other states. Following incidence rate trends, Florida has the second highest mortality rate. Since more incidences of cancer most likely result in more deaths, it makes sense that California and Florida take the top two spots for deaths caused by cancer.
Each map uses the same scale for easier representation of results.




Statistical Tests

An ANOVA test was performed using Python's SciPy library on incidence and mortality rate. This test was used to compare the variances in the mean between male and female incidence rate, along with male and female mortality rate. Examining the p-value (shown below) of these results can be used to compare determine if a statistical difference is present between male and female rates of cancer, and deaths due to cancer. Two hypothesis exist for the data I have presented; these are shown below.
Null hypothesis 1: Male and female incidence rates are statistically similar
Null hypothesis 2: Male and female mortality rates are statistically similar

When looking at the data sets for incidence and mortality between men and women, the number of people in each set is not extremely different. From 1999 to 2016, slightly more men have both been diagnosed with cancer and died from it. This is a reasonable outcome, as there are slightly more males in the population than females (Smirnova).

The results of each test can be seen in the ANOVA Test table. The p-value for both incidence rate and mortality is far above 0.05; because of this, it can be concluded that no statistical difference exists in the incidence or death of cancer between men and women.
An error that may come into play for the ANOVA test is that the rates of cancer may not be normally distributed; if the average age of incidence is significantly different, the ANOVA test is not suitable for analysis.
